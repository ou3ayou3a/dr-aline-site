How I Practice

Clinical Philosophy

"The art of endodontics is knowing when to intervene and when to preserve — and having the precision to execute both with equal conviction."

Principle One

Evidence Over Convention

Every treatment decision in this practice is grounded in peer-reviewed evidence, not habit or tradition.

Endodontics has evolved dramatically in the last two decades — from the materials we use to the way we visualise canal anatomy. Dr. Aline stays at the frontier of this evolution through continuous engagement with the literature, participation in research, and attendance at international conferences. When a new bioceramic sealer or obturation technique shows superior outcomes in controlled studies, it enters the protocol. When a legacy method no longer holds up to scrutiny, it's retired — regardless of how long it's been in use.

Principle Two

Microscopic Precision

The operating microscope is not an accessory — it is the lens through which every case is diagnosed and treated.

Magnification reveals what the naked eye cannot: the fourth canal in a maxillary molar, a hairline fracture in a posterior tooth, the distinction between a ledge and a natural curve. Dr. Aline works under the dental operating microscope for every procedure — not selectively, not for complex cases only, but as the default standard. This level of visualisation, combined with CBCT three-dimensional imaging, allows for treatment plans built on what is actually there, not what is assumed.

Principle Three

Preserve Whenever Possible

Extraction is a last resort. A well-treated natural tooth can serve you for life.

There is a growing trend in dentistry toward extraction and implant placement, sometimes prematurely. Dr. Aline takes a preservation-first approach: if a tooth can be saved through endodontic treatment — even retreatment or surgery — it should be. The natural tooth, with its proprioceptive feedback and biological integration, offers advantages that no prosthetic replacement can fully replicate. This philosophy does not mean treating hopeless teeth; it means giving treatable teeth the chance they deserve.

Principle Four

Patient-Centered Communication

Informed consent is not a form to sign — it is a conversation to have.

Many patients arrive with anxiety about root canal treatment, often driven by outdated perceptions. Dr. Aline takes the time to explain the diagnosis, the treatment options, and the expected outcomes — in language that makes sense. Visual aids from the operating microscope help patients understand their own anatomy and the rationale behind the recommended treatment. This transparency builds trust and reduces anxiety, leading to better experiences and better outcomes.
